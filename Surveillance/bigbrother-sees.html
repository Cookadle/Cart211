Big Brother Sees All

The concept of "Big Brother" is a reference to George Orwell's 1984, where an authoritarian government monitors every action of its citizens—has evolved in the digital world.
Today, it’s not just governments that are watching us;
it’s the apps we use daily. Whether it's social media platforms,
fitness trackers, or even weather apps, almost every application
you interact with is collecting data about you.
This data can range from your location, browsing habits, and purchase history, to even more sensitive information like health data, personal preferences, and private conversations.

In the digital age, our personal data is one of the most valuable currencies on the internet. From the moment we wake up and unlock our phones to the time we fall asleep, we are constantly interacting with a vast network of applications, each designed to make our lives easier, more convenient, or even more entertaining. But behind this convenience lies a disturbing truth: these apps are watching us, collecting data, and tracking our every move, often without our explicit consent.

Silent surveillance

Many of these apps are equipped with built-in tracking features that silently monitor your activity in the background. Some apps track your location, even when you aren’t using them. Others analyze your in-app behaviors to personalize ads, or to suggest products, services, and content. But what many users don’t realize is how much of their data is being collected, and how little control they have over it. A large portion of this surveillance occurs without our explicit knowledge. Sure, we see the "Terms and Conditions" and "Privacy Policy" pop-ups when we first download an app, but how many of us actually read these? Often, these agreements are lengthy, filled with legal jargon, and presented in a way that makes opting out seem like a daunting task. By accepting these terms, we give our consent to being tracked, sometimes in ways we don’t fully understand.

How Apps Are Tracking Your Data

Most modern apps request access to your location, even if they don’t necessarily need it to function. This location data is often tracked in the background, meaning your movements are monitored even when you’re not actively using the app. For example, apps like Google Maps, Facebook, and Instagram track your location and store your location history to offer "local" recommendations, advertisements, and services. This location data is valuable not only for improving app services but also for advertisers and marketers who want to target you based on where you go and what you do.

How you see it in real-life : Have you ever noticed that after you visit a specific store, you start seeing ads for that store or similar products in your feed? That’s because your location data has been tracked, analyzed, and used to target you with hyper-localized ads.

Behavioral Data

Apps track everything you do inside them—what you click, how long you spend on a page, which buttons you press, and which content you interact with. This data is used to build a profile of you, creating a digital footprint that reveals your habits, preferences, and even your emotional state. Social media apps like Facebook and Instagram track your likes, comments, and shares to predict what you’re likely to engage with next. E-commerce apps like Amazon use your browsing and purchase history to recommend products tailored specifically to you.

How you see it in real-life : If you’ve ever noticed that after researching a product online, ads for that product follow you around the internet, you’ve experienced the power of behavioral data. This data is used to serve you with targeted ads designed to keep you engaged and increase your likelihood of making a purchase.

Facial Recognition

Some apps use facial recognition technology to identify you and create a personalized experience. While it can be used for convenience, such as unlocking your phone or tagging photos in social media apps, it’s also a powerful surveillance tool. Apps like Facebook use facial recognition to automatically identify people in photos, which, unbeknownst to many users, can be stored for future use.

How you see it in real-life : Facial recognition technology has been used in retail environments to track customer behavior and make targeted offers. The technology can also be used to monitor your emotions or reactions to advertisements, further personalizing your experience.

The power lies in our hands — to either accept the trade-off or take steps to protect our personal information.
