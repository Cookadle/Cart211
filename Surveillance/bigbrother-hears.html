Big Brother Hears All

Insights into audio surveillance and how your conversation are used for money.

Some apps, especially voice assistants like Siri, Alexa, and Google Assistant, are designed to listen to your voice in order to respond to commands. But these devices often continue to listen to your surroundings even when you aren’t interacting with them Welcome to the age where "Big Brother" not only sees all but hears everything.

The Rise of Voice Assistants

Voice assistants, once seen as a futuristic luxury, have become a staple in modern households. Devices like Amazon Echo, Google Home, and Apple’s Siri allow users to control their environment with just their voice. From asking for the weather forecast to playing music or turning off the lights, these devices seem to make life more convenient. However, there’s a catch. These devices are always listening, recording snippets of conversation that might not even involve direct interaction with the device.

But the listening doesn't stop once the device has been activated. These voice assistants are always "on," recording snippets of conversation that might not even involve direct interaction with the device. This constant surveillance raises significant privacy concerns, as tech companies collect, store, and analyze audio data from users, often without their full awareness or consent.

How do these devices listen to us?

To understand the issue, we need to look at how voice assistants work. These devices are equipped with advanced microphones that are designed to listen for specific trigger words. But once they detect the trigger, they begin recording. The data is then sent to the company’s servers, where it is processed, analyzed, and stored for improving services or offering targeted ads.

In some cases, the device continues listening even after it’s not activated, storing fragments of conversations in the background. While these recordings are intended to improve the accuracy of voice recognition, they also raise the question: how much data is being collected, and what is being done with it?

The dark side of always listening devices

The biggest concern with voice assistants is the constant collection of audio data. Even when you’re not interacting with the device, it’s listening to everything around it. This means sensitive conversations—like personal, political, or even private business discussions—could be inadvertently captured and stored by these devices.

For example, after discussing a product or service in front of your smart speaker, you may notice ads related to that conversation appearing on your phone or computer. This is due to a process known as targeted advertising, where voice data is used to tailor advertisements based on your preferences, activities, and even the things you talk about. Though tech companies claim that this data is anonymized and used to improve their services, the reality is that such data can easily be used for profiling and surveillance.

Ads Following Your Conversations

Have you ever had a conversation about a product or service, and shortly afterward, you start seeing ads for that exact thing on your phone or computer? This might seem like a coincidence, but it’s not. Many users have reported the strange phenomenon of “listening” ads, where an app or voice assistant seems to know things they’ve only discussed out loud. While companies like Amazon and Google deny using voice recordings for ad targeting, the reality is that these voice assistants capture a wealth of data from the conversations they overhear.

How you see it in real-life : There have been numerous reports of users noticing that ads related to something they just talked about seem to appear out of nowhere. This leads many to believe that voice assistants are listening to private conversations, collecting data even when the app isn’t actively engaged.

The Role of Third Parties in Voice Data Collection

It’s not just the tech companies themselves that have access to your voice data. Many voice assistant systems share this data with third-party developers who create skills or apps for these devices. These third parties often have access to the voice data you provide and can use it for their own purposes—whether it’s for analytics, improving their app, or even selling that data to other marketers.

In 2019, reports surfaced that contractors at Amazon were listening to users' Alexa recordings in order to improve the device's response accuracy. This raised ethical concerns about the privacy of users' conversations, and though Amazon implemented safeguards and policies to limit human access to recordings, the issue remains a significant concern.

As consumers and citizens, we are not powerless.
